Bio

I'm a forest ecologist, focused on forest adaptation under climate change. My work? Assessing impacts, creating adaptive strategies, and using niche modeling to predict changes. Being trained in data science, I apply statistics and machine learning to build interdisciplinary models. The objective is simple - understand the impact of climate change on forest ecosystems and trees, identify conservation priorities, and finding home for forests in the future.

Scale-free climate variables generated by ClimateAP outperforms widely used gridded data

  • Hypothesis 1: ClimateAP achieves higher accuracy than WorldClim

    Regress observed climate on ClimateAP and WorldClim predictions

    Compare results from ClimateAP regressions to those of WorldClim regressions

    Result:

    • Both sources provide accurate data

    • In general, ClimateAP is more accurate than WorldClim in predicting precipitation

  • Hypothesis 2: ClimateAP shows more advantage in mountainous areas

    Classify landforms into 2 groups using Topographic Position Index (TPI) and Slope

    Regress observed climate on ClimateAP and WorldClim predictions by landform group

    Compare regression results of ClimateAP and WorldClim regressions within each landform group

    Result:

    • ClimateAP is more accurate than WorldClim on complex landforms (Landform 1-4 & 6-10 from Weiss (2001))

  • Hypothesis 3: Quality of Niche Models built with ClimateAP is higher than those built with WorldClim

    Construct two climatic niche models for Cunningham lanceolata, one using ClimateAPv3.01 data and the other using WorldClim1.4 data

    Evaluate models based on OOB errors, predictor’s & user‘s accuracy, Kappa statistics, ROC-AUC

    Compare the performance of the model based on ClimateAP data to that of the model based on WorldClim data, and ascertain which dataset provides superior model performance

    Results:

    • Data from ClimateAP contributes to a slightly more accurate niche model

Projecting the Suitable habitat for forest ecosystems

The urgency of climate change is magnified by extreme weather events and ecosystem shifts, with changes in climatic niches of up to 77% reported. Ecosystem classification is key for effective conservation and forest management, reducing the need for frequent site visits and enabling the development of tailored strategies. This study investigates the link between climate, soil, and forest ecosystems, forecasting future habitats and possible new ecosystems under diverse climate scenarios. The results aim to inform effective biodiversity conservation in the face of accelerating climate change.

Fact about climate change:

Human activities are unequivocally causing global warming. We are already seeing widespread and rapid changes in the Earth's climate system, affecting many weather and climate extremes in every region across the globe. If global temperatures increase by 2 degrees Celsius, heat extremes would often reach critical tolerance thresholds for agriculture and health, severely impacting human livelihoods and lives.
